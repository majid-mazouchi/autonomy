From Method to Product

Nine posts built the method. This one turns it into software — a multi-agent system, with tools, memory, and humans in the loop, that diagnoses a fleet at scale without inventing its answers.

By Majid Mazouchi  ·  Vehicle Health Monitoring Notes
Abstract

A method is something a careful person follows; a product is software that runs that method at fleet scale, every day, on live data — for users who will stake a recall on its answer. That last clause is the whole design problem: the tool must produce a defensible, reproducible verdict with a human on the hook for it. This post lays out the architecture in plain words — seven planes (data, detection, reasoning, tools, memory, runtime, human-in-the-loop) wired by one rule: agents fetch and propose, deterministic tools verify, humans commit. We cover the always-on detection layer that turns a firehose into a ranked queue of cases, the multi-agent reasoning engine (an orchestrator over typed worker agents — not a free-form swarm), MCP-first tools with reads free and writes gated, five kinds of memory with strict write-governance, and the parts demos skip: evals, guardrails, observability, cost, multi-tenancy, and the closed loop that makes it smarter per fleet over time.

§ 01 — From a Method to a Machine

One rule shapes the whole design: trust under audit

A person doing diagnosis can be careful, explain themselves, and be questioned. Software doing it for a whole fleet has to be all of those at once, on demand, with a paper trail — because its answer can move money and safety.

So the goal is not a clever chatbot. It is a system whose every verdict comes with its evidence, its calculations, and a human who approved it. Five principles fall out of that, and they are the same ones the rest of this series has been building toward:

  • Deterministic tools where they belong; the model where it earns its keep. The LLM never computes a p-value, a survival curve, or a rate — it calls a tool that does, or it says it can’t. The model perceives, proposes hypotheses, orchestrates, and explains. This one rule removes most of the hallucination risk that would otherwise make the product unshippable.
  • Cheap detection, expensive reasoning — and reasoning runs per case, not per datapoint. Always-on statistics watch the whole fleet for pennies; the agent team is woken only when a case is opened. This is the difference between a sane bill and an absurd one at fleet scale.
  • Humans gate irreversible steps. Agents fetch and propose; humans commit. Gates before concluding, before proposing a fix, before scoping a campaign.
  • Everything is auditable and eval-gated. Each conclusion carries its trail; nothing graduates to “automatic” until it beats a held-out test set.
  • Ingested text is untrusted. Service notes, bulletins, and complaints can carry hidden instructions. They are data to reason about, never commands to follow.
The one rule, in six words

Agents propose, tools verify, humans commit. If a feature breaks that sentence, it does not ship.

Part A — The Planes

Seven layers, each with one job

The system is a stack. Each plane does one thing, scales on its own, and is owned by a different kind of engineer. Build them in order; never let one plane do another’s job.

§ 02 — The Whole Thing in One Diagram

How the planes stack

Data flows up the stack; a finished, human-approved verdict comes out the top; and confirmed results loop back down to make the next diagnosis smarter.

§ 03 — The Data Plane

From a firehose to one clean table

Nothing above works without joined, aligned, trustworthy data. This is the unglamorous foundation — and the hardest part to fake.

Raw signals arrive from everywhere: telemetry and codes streaming off vehicles, build and supplier records, warranty claims, service notes, and documents. The data plane turns that mess into one row per vehicle and a few shared lookups the rest of the system can trust.

§ 04 — The Detection Plane

Turn the firehose into a queue of cases

You do not run an expensive agent team on every reading. You run cheap, always-on monitors on everything, and wake the agents only when something is worth investigating.

This is the cost governor and the reason the product is proactive instead of a chatbot you have to prod. The monitors are the fleet-rigor methods running live: per-cohort CUSUM, change-point on prevalence, anomaly and drift. When one trips, a case is opened — the unit of work the rest of the system reasons about — and triage ranks it by safety, how fast it is spreading, and cost.

Algorithm 1open_case(signal)

for each cohort monitor that fired:

if duplicate of an open case: attach evidence; continue

score ← safety × spread_rate × cost_exposure # triage priority

case ← new Case(cohort, signals, score)

enqueue(case) # agents pull by score

# agents never run on raw data — only on a triaged case

Part B — The Reasoning Engine

The agents, the loop, the tools, the memory

This is the part everyone pictures when they hear “AI agent.” The trick is to make it boring, typed, and resumable — so it can be trusted and audited.

§ 05 — The Agent Team (Not a Swarm)

An orchestrator over typed workers, sharing one notebook

The tempting design is a chatroom of agents talking until they agree. It does not survive production: unpredictable, unauditable, expensive. The shape that works is an orchestrator that hands typed jobs to worker agents, all reading and writing one shared blackboard (the case’s state).

§ 06 — The Diagnostic Loop

One case, start to finish

Here is the whole investigation as a flowchart. It is the method from the rest of the series, wired with gates and loops so the system can say “not yet” instead of guessing.

Algorithm 2diagnose_case(case)

plan ← orchestrator.plan(case) # which workers, order, budget

loop:

perceive(case); ground(case) # signals + memory + docs

H ← hypothesize(case)

for h in H: analyze(h, TOOLS) # stats / physics — never the model itself

state ← critic.gate(case) # sufficiency · impostor · confounding

if state = INCONCLUSIVE: collect(next_test); continue

if state = INCOHERENT: reframe(); continue

break

verdict ← synthesize(case) # chain · confidence · kill-switch

if not human.approve(verdict): revise(); return diagnose_case(case)

act(verdict, gated=true); schedule(verify(case)) # verify the curve bends

§ 07 — Tools, MCP-First

Reads are free; writes go through a human

Every capability is a tool the agents call — exposed over the Model Context Protocol so it is discoverable, versioned, permissioned, and reusable (the same tools power your editor copilot). The split that matters: read tools are open; write tools are gated.

§ 08 — Memory: Five Kinds

Different memories for different jobs — and a guard on what gets remembered

“Memory” is not one thing. A practical system keeps five, each with its own purpose and its own rule for what may be written.

Algorithm 3remember(fact)

write working[case] # always — ephemeral scratchpad

if fact.kind = "case outcome": write episodic # grow the case library

if fact.human_confirmed and fact.verified:

promote → semantic / long-term # only confirmed facts graduate

update detection_priors(fact) # next diagnosis starts smarter

else: keep episodic with provenance + TTL # never trust an unconfirmed guess

Part C — Making It a Product

The parts demos skip

A weekend demo ends at “the agent found the bug.” A product has to be affordable, trustworthy, auditable, multi-tenant, and able to get better over time. That is most of the work.

§ 09 — Runtime & Cost

Make it reliable, resumable, and affordable

A diagnosis is a long-running thing — it can pause for days waiting on a human gate or for new data to arrive. The runtime has to survive that.

Three pieces carry it. A durable workflow engine runs each case as a resumable program: if a worker fails, it retries; if it waits on a human, it sleeps and wakes — no lost state. A budget governor caps the tokens and compute a case may spend, so one hard case can’t run away with the bill. And a model router sends easy steps (routing, summarizing) to a small cheap model and saves the frontier model for the hard reasoning — hypothesizing and the critic. Combined with “detection cheap, reasoning per case,” this is what keeps cost growing with the number of real problems, not with the size of the fleet.

§ 10 — Trust

The hardest subsystem: proving it’s right

You cannot ship “trust me.” The thing that turns a clever pipeline into a product people stake decisions on is the machinery that measures whether it works — and it is also your deepest moat, because competitors can copy the agents but not your evidence.

Evals — the core asset

does it get the right answer?

Grade the pipeline against labeled past cases and synthetic ground-truth fleets with known injected causes (the same idea as the sample-evidence pack): did it recover the cause, the cohort, the Simpson collapse, the CUSUM alarm? Plus confidence calibration — when it says 90%, is it right 90% of the time?

Guardrails

stop the foreseeable failures

The model can’t invent statistics (tool-enforced), can’t take an irreversible action without a gate, and treats ingested documents as untrusted (prompt-injection defense). Outputs are schema-validated.

Observability

see what it did

Tracing across every agent and tool call, with cost, latency, tool-success and groundedness metrics, and drift monitors on the detectors. When something goes wrong you can replay the exact case.

Audit

a feature, not plumbing

Every verdict links to its evidence, the tool outputs it used, and the human who approved it — immutable. In a regulated, recall-driven domain the audit trail is the headline feature.

The trap to design against

Eval debt: shipping autonomy you can’t measure. Build the eval harness before you automate a step, not after — otherwise you can never safely take the human out of any loop.

§ 11 — The Closed Loop & Scale

It gets smarter per fleet over time

The last move is what separates a tool from a system: every confirmed, verified case feeds back and makes the next diagnosis faster, cheaper, and earlier.

Scaling the rest is conventional once the planes are separated: the data plane scales on big-data infrastructure, the agent plane scales as stateless workers behind the durable engine, and tools are independent services. Multi-tenancy isolates each OEM/fleet (memory and the knowledge graph are tenant-scoped, with optional shared, anonymized priors). And because recalls are regulated, compliance — data residency (some OEMs require on-prem/VPC), access control, encryption, and immutable audit — is part of the architecture from day one, not bolted on.

§ 12 — A Build Path

Don’t build it all at once

Ship value before autonomy. The fastest road to a trusted product is a useful assistant that earns the right to take the wheel.

1
the wedge

Data plane + detection + a human-driven copilot

For one design-partner fleet: the clean data table, the always-on monitors, and an assistant that helps an engineer investigate using the deterministic stats tools. Seed the case library. Ship value before any autonomy.

2
automate with gates

The multi-agent engine + HITL + the eval harness

Add the orchestrator and worker agents, the knowledge graph and episodic memory, and — critically — the eval harness on synthetic ground-truth fleets, so you can prove each automated step before trusting it.

3
the platform

The closed loop, multi-tenancy, compliance, scale

Turn on the feedback flywheel (detector tuning + CAPA), make it multi-tenant and compliant, scale out, and embed the copilot in the tools engineers already use.

Four risks to design against from day one

Eval debt (autonomy you can’t measure), memory poisoning (ungoverned writes), cost blow-up (running agents on data instead of cases), and trust collapse (one confident wrong recall). Every one is prevented by a principle from §01 — which is why they are principles, not features.

§ 15 — The Cost Model

Why the bill stays small

The whole point of “cheap detection, reasoning per case” is the economics. Here it is on the back of an envelope, for a million-car fleet.

Detection runs cheap statistics on per-vehicle summaries — roughly $0.0001 per car-day. A million cars is $100/day ≈ $3,000/month, and it grows gently with fleet size. Reasoning only fires on real cases — a healthy fleet has on the order of ~150 distinct issues a month, not millions — at maybe $8 of model + tool compute each (after model routing), so about $1,200/month, and it grows with the number of real problems, not the fleet. Total: roughly $4,200/month for a million cars.

Contrast the naïve design — run an LLM over every car every day: 1,000,000 × 30 × ~$0.02 ≈ $600,000/month, for a worse, unauditable answer.

§ 17 — Practical Notes

Field rules for building the system

1
the one rule

Agents propose, tools verify, humans commit

If a feature breaks that sentence, it doesn’t ship. It is the spine of every decision below.

2
cheap vs costly

Detect on everything, reason per case

Always-on statistics scan the fleet for pennies; wake the agents only for a triaged case. This keeps cost tied to real problems, not fleet size.

3
tools compute

Never let the model do arithmetic

Every number comes from a deterministic tool or not at all. This single rule removes most hallucination risk.

4
typed, not chatty

Orchestrator + blackboard + durable execution — not a swarm

Typed hand-offs and a resumable workflow give you reproducibility, auditability, and the ability to pause for days at a gate.

5
gate the writes

Reads free, writes through a human

Read tools are open; anything that files a campaign, opens a ticket, or changes a detector passes the human gate.

6
govern memory

Only confirmed facts graduate

Working and episodic memory record everything with provenance; only human-confirmed, verified results enter durable memory and the priors. Otherwise the system poisons itself.

7
evals first

Build the test harness before you automate

Synthetic ground-truth fleets + labeled cases + calibration. You can only remove a human from a loop you can measure.

8
copilot before autopilot

Earn autonomy in phases

Ship a useful assistant first; let it prove itself on real cases; widen its autonomy only where the evals justify it.

§ 18 — References & Further Reading

Where these ideas come from

This architecture braids agent design, data systems, and ML-in-production practice onto the diagnosis method of the series. Entry points, grouped.

Agents & multi-agent systems

S. Russell & P. Norvig, Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach (4th ed., 2020). The agent abstraction — perception, action, environment — that the whole reasoning plane rests on.

M. Wooldridge, An Introduction to MultiAgent Systems (2nd ed., 2009). Coordination, roles, and hand-offs between cooperating agents.

Anthropic, “Building Effective Agents” (2024). Practical patterns — orchestrator-workers, evaluator-optimizer — and when not to use an agent.

Model Context Protocol (MCP) — open specification (2024). The standard for exposing tools and data to agents that the tool plane is built on.

Reasoning, retrieval & diagnosis

P. Lewis et al., “Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks” (2020). Grounding the model in retrieved documents and prior cases.

A. Hogan et al., “Knowledge Graphs” (2021). The graph of parts, suppliers, software, faults, and fixes that is the system’s semantic memory.

R. Reiter (1987); J. de Kleer & B. C. Williams (1987). Consistency-based, multiple-fault diagnosis — the formal backbone of the method the agents run.

Data & streaming systems

M. Kleppmann, Designing Data-Intensive Applications (2017). The reliability, scalability, and maintainability foundations of the data plane.

T. Akidau et al., “The Dataflow Model” (2015). Correct stream processing — the basis of always-on detection.

R. Kimball & M. Ross, The Data Warehouse Toolkit (3rd ed., 2013). Dimensional modelling behind the gold, one-row-per-vehicle table.

Orchestration, ML-in-production & safety

G. Hohpe & B. Woolf, Enterprise Integration Patterns (2003). Messaging and orchestration patterns underneath durable, long-running workflows.

D. Sculley et al., “Hidden Technical Debt in Machine Learning Systems” (2015). Why the model is the small part — and the origin of “eval debt.”

L. Zheng et al., “Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena” (2023). Automated evaluation of model outputs, with its pitfalls.

OWASP, “Top 10 for LLM Applications.” Prompt injection and agent-action risks — the basis of the guardrails, especially for untrusted ingested text.

A note on these references

These are orientation points into large literatures, cited to show the lineage of the design rather than as the source of any single claim here. Titles and years are given so you can find them.
